Lesson 1: Scales

Getting Started: Welcome to my tutorial on improvisation. I am honored that you have taken this time with me to explore the art of composing music in the moment. My intent in this tutorial is to share only the information you absolutely need to get started creating original solos, however, in my attempt to simplify there may at times be too little information. If this happens and you begin to feel lost on your musical journey please feel free to click the Book a Lesson button at the bottom the page for a little extra help. Now let's get started.

The Chromatic Scale: The chromatic scale is the foundation to all improvisation. I once heard a jazz musician say, if you play a note you don't like, you're only a half step away from one you will like. The first step in learning to improvise is mastering your chromatic scale. I like to teach the chromatic scale with sharps going up and flats coming down. Here is the complete scale up and down beginning on C: Up: C-C#-D-D#-E-F-F#-G-G#-A-A#-B-C and Down: C-B-Bb-A-Ab-G-Gb-F-E-Eb-D-Db-C

Major Scale Formula: Once you've mastered your chromatic scale, you're ready to move on to the major scale. There are multiple ways to learn your major scales. Some musicians learn them by learning the flats or sharps in each scale. Others pick them out by ear and memorize them. I like to use the major scale formula. If we let R = Root, WS = Whole Step, and HS = Half Step, the formula is: R WS WS HS WS WS WS HS. The root is the first note, so if, for example, you're trying to find the Eb scale, Eb is the root. From the Root, we move by whole steps and half steps until we arrive back at the root up an octave. A half step is a movement to the very next note of the chromatic scale. A whole step skips the very next note and plays the note after. If this seems confusing, studying the Major Scale chart may be helpful.

15 Scales Instead of 12: How many major scales are there? My guess is that most musicians would say 12, but there are actually 15 when we're thinking about improvisation. Let's look at Db for example. We can think of this scale as the Db scale or the C# scale. The fingering is the same, so why would we practice both? When we're in the key of E, it's quite likely that we will run across a C# chord somewhere in the song. If we've only practiced this scale as Db, thinking of it as C# may feel a little uncomfortable in the moment.

All of this being said, the charts in this tutorial only have 14 keys; I have omitted Cb. I did this partially because the people that designed my chord speller did not allow for Cb chords as a search option and partially because I've never seen anyone put a Cb chord on a lead sheet. If you have, please let me know.

E#, Fb, B#, and Cb: This may be an good time to point out that on the Major Scale chart, I use the theoretically correct names for the notes E#, B# and Cb, but in the next lesson and throughout the rest of the course, I will be using F for E#, E for Fb, C for B# and B for Cb.

Lesson 2: Triad Chords

Creating a Solo with Chord Tones: The best way to get started improvising is to play chord tones. Let's take a look at a simple chord progression in the key of C: Em-Am-Dm-G. Assume the song is in 4/4 time and each chord lasts one measure. To keep things simple, we will play 4 quarter notes for each measure. To build a minor chord from our major scale we use the root, the 3rd lowered a half step, and the 5th. Referring back to the Major Scales chart, you can see the notes for an Em chord are E, G (G# lowered a half step), and B. We need a fourth note, so we'll add E up an octave from the first E as our fourth note. Continuing in this manner, we would play; A, C, E, A for the Am chord; D, F, A, D for the Dm chord and G, B, D, G for the G chord. Our solo from start to finish would be: E, G, B, E, A, C, E, A, D, F, A, D, G, B, D, G. This may be a good time to click the Book a Lesson button at the bottom of the page.

Trying It Out: To get started you'll need the lead sheet to a song that you already know well on your instrument. I recommend using a song in 4/4 time with chord changes that last an entire measure. You will probably need to simplify the chords to their triad form for now. In a later lesson, we will talk about seventh chords and other more complex chords.

Although improvising is done in the moment, for this first exercise, we are going to write out every note like we did in the example above for the Em-Am-Dm-G progression. I recommend you write out the chord tones for only four measures at a time and then stop and play through what you've written until it's fluid. Unless you're a bass player, this won't be what you'll actually play when you improvise, but it's the foundation you will use to build your solos.

Lesson 3: Patterns

In the Practice Room: If you're like most students, writing out the chord tones for that last exercise was a lot of work, and you probably came away thinking, This is definitely not something that I could do in the moment performing in front of an audience. Advanced musicians make improvising look natural and effortless, but don't be fooled, they put a lot of hours in before they ever stepped in front of an audience. It takes time to create the muscle memory that makes soloing appear smooth and easy.

The Circle of 5ths: I recommend using the circle of 5ths to practice the triad chords we've learned so far. Start on C and play the 1-3-5-8 pattern for your major, minor, diminished, and augmented chords in that key. Next move to F, the next scale to the left on the circle of 5ths and work the same pattern for the major, minor, diminished, and augmented chords. Continue on to Bb, Eb, Ab, Db and Gb. Remember Gb is also F#. Practice that scale first thinking of the notes for Gb and then the notes for F#. Now continue on through the sharp keys: B, E, A, D, and G.

Trying a New Pattern: Once you feel comfortable with the 1-3-5-8 pattern, try something new like 1-8-5-3. Work through the circle of 5ths using this pattern until it's comfortable for all 12 chords. Once both of those patterns feel smooth, try alternating between the two patterns. Some other patterns to try are 1-3-5-3 and 3-1-3-5. Once you are comfortable alternating through a variety of patterns, you are ready to move to the next section where we begin to add in the passing tones 2 and 6.

Lesson 4: Pentatonic

The Pentatonic Scale: Let's take some time to look at an easy scale that's great for soloing. For a moment we'll put aside what we've learned about chords and work exclusively with the pentatonic scale. Going back to the major scale, the formula for the pentatonic scale is: 1-2-3-5-6. Instead of changing with each new chord, we will use just this one scale for the entire song, so if the song is in the key of F, use F-G-A-C-D to build a solo no matter where the song is in the chord progression.

Lesson 5: 7th Chords

Adding 7ths: I'm sure you've noticed that rarely do you see simply major, minor, diminished, augmented chords on your lead sheets. No doubt those lead sheets are filled with 7ths, 9ths, and numerous other unfamiliar additions. In this lesson, we'll learn the sevenths. To build a major 7th (maj7) we add the 7 from the major scale to the 1-3-5 for the major chord, making the maj7 formula: 1-3-5-7. If a lead sheet adds 7 to a chord without the "maj" or "m", this is what's called a dominant 7th. For this chord, we will take that same b7 and add it to the 1-3-5 for the major chord, making the dominant 7 formula: 1-3-5-b7. For the minor 7th, we will from the major scale a half step (b7) and then add it to the 1-b3-5 for the minor chord, making the m7 formula: 1-b3-5-b7. The next seventh chord we will learn is the m7b5, which is built by adding the b7 to the 1-b3-b5 for the diminished chord, making the m7b5 formula: 1-b3-b5-b7. the final seventh chord we will learn is the diminished 7th. You might be thinking, wait, didn't we just add a 7th to the diminished chord? Yes, we did, but that chord goes by a different name. To build a diminished seventh, we must also diminish the 7th, which means we need to lower that b7 down another half step to a bb7, making the dim7 formula: 1-b3-b5-bb7.

Lesson 6: More Chords

8th and Beyond: If we continue our major scale for a second octave, the numbering system continues on to 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, and 13. For the C scale, this would make C-8, D-9, E-10, F-11, G-12, and A-13. It's important to note that we don't actually refer to the 10 or the 12 when naming chords.

9th Chords: Building a 9th chord is as simple as adding the 9 to the appropriate 7th chord. If you are building a maj9, you take the 1-3-5-7 for the maj7 and add the 9th to make the maj9 formula: 1-3-5-7-9. For a min9, you take the 1-b3-5-b7 for the m7 and add the 9th to make the m9 formula: 1-b3-5-b7-9.

Lesson 7: Transcription

The Importance of Transcription: We have finally arrived at the end of our tutorial. Hopefully, I've given you a lot of information to experiment with as you explore the world of improvisation. But before we go, I want to leave you with some advice from one of my favorite musicians, my son. When I asked him, how do you teach someone to improvise, his immediate unfiltered response was this: Play scales and chords, listen, transcribe, listen some more, transcribe some more, play more scales and chords. That pretty much sums it up.

Starting to Transcribe: So what is transcription? Transcription is a fancy word for put what you hear on paper. Turn on one of your favoirite recordings and listen. Listen until you hear a snippet of improvisation that inspires you. Now slow down the recording and figure out those notes. It will be work, but it will be worth it. Next, write them down somewhere, preferably on your heart or in your soul, but also on paper or in the space I've provided on this page. This is transcription. If you do it enough it will change your playing forever. Now go and play your scales and chords, listen, transcribe and most of all always remember the quote at the top of the page: Do not fear mistakes. There are none.
